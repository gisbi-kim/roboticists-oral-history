## About Richard Volz

IEEE Fellow, Richard Volz, was born on 10 July 1937 in Woodstock, Illinois. He has received a Decoration for Exceptional Civilian Service from the United States Air Force; a Public Service Award from NASA; an appreciation plaque from the NASA astronauts, and the Millennium Medal and the Robotics and Automation Society Distinguished Service Award from IEEE. 

After graduating from high school in Woodstock, he enrolled at Northwestern University. Volz started as a math major, but funding at the department ran out and he found funding at the engineering co-opt program. Scholarships enabled him to continue his education, he selected electrical engineering for two reasons, primarily because he originally thought it was more math involved, and secondly, he had built his own amateur radio and had a radio license and thought it was a natural fit. After his undergraduate studies, Volz would stay at Northwestern where he would complete both his masters and Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering. 

In 1964, Volz took a job at the University of Michigan (1964-1989), professor of electrical engineering where he became part of the control, which control activity at Michigan was spread across several departments. This became his home department because the interdepartmental program systems engineering lab was housed adjacent to the Electrical Engineering department. When he became interested in using computer systems for helping to design controls, Volz worked on building a couple of different systems that were shipped to many different places around the world to help design control systems that would input things in a more natural language. The systems would be sent to Stanford, Rensselaer, along with other universities in England, Spain, and even Australia. Volz was on the University Committee of Computer Utilization where he would be asked to be an associate department head at Michigan, and then associate computing center director at Michigan. 

Shortly after the Challenger explosion, in 1986, Volz joined a NASA panel appointed to review everything about the shuttle, the cause of the disaster, and plans for future shuttle spacecrafts. 

Volz’s role was to look into the general-purpose computers and see which computers need to be replaced if not all of them. Volz would then move on to work for both NASA and Texas A&M where he would work on a series of projects and systems for NASA, along with being in the Space Station Advisory board and serve as the department head of computer science. 

Along with being on four panels between NASA and Texas A&M, Volz also worked closely with IEEE, serving president of the IEEE Robotics and Automation Society from 2006-2007, along with being an editor for the Transactions on Robotics and Automation for IEEE and the Robotics and Automation Society startup. In 1994, he was asked to be editor-in-chief of the transactions, and in 1999 he became the vice president for publications where he would then move onto being the president from 2004-2006. He also was a member of the IEEE Technical Activities Board (TAB), the IEEE Conference Committee, and the IEEE Finance Committee. 

Richard Volz has received many awards and honors including being able to win a National Science Foundation Fellowship for three years for his graduate studies. 

He was elected president of several societies, including Beta Kappa Nu, Tau Beta Pi, and has received many awards, including the Eshbach Award at Northwestern Technological Institute, 1960; the IRE Outstanding Student Award, 1960; and the Cabell Fellowship at Northwestern Technological Institute, 1963-64; the Eta Kappa Nu Outstanding Teacher Award from the University of Michigan, 1975; NASA Special Service Award, 1993; and the Decoration for Exceptional Civilian Service from the U.S. Air Force, 1994; NASA Special Service Award, 1993.

Now, Tom Sheridan initiated some of that at MIT and what we were looking at and not just – when you’re controlling it, you’re controlling the simulation. There’s not any fundamental reason why the simulation has to stay in time synchrony with the real robot. And so you could be controlling the simulation and do it faster and the real robot moves along the path that you’re doing, but slower. And then you can do other interesting things like if you see that you made a mistake, you can back up and redo it and sometimes for final positioning you want to move things around and get it in position before you do the final move, where you could kind of clip all those out with what we call a “position clutch” and that was kind of the idea behind that.
