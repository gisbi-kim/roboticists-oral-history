# Ralph Hollis — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/hollis.md`)

```
1941                — Born in Hutchinson, Kansas; later grew up in Wichita
Around 1954 (age 13) — Read W. Grey Walter's Scientific American articles (1951, 1953) and became interested in robots; tried building a capacitive-sensing robot with friend Phil Roberts (failed due to lead-acid battery weight)
Around 1957         — Built a cylindrical wall-following robot from an Erector set and telephone relays (passed through automatic doors)
Around 1959         — Before entering University of Wichita, started programming the IBM 610 computer in high school
1959~1961           — Two years majoring in mathematics at University of Wichita; learned Bendix G-15 computer; used the G-15 at Beech Aircraft during night shifts (rocket calculations)
Subsequent studies  — Transferred to Kansas State University, B.S. and M.S. in Physics
1965~1970           — Joined California North American Aviation — led an 8-person team simulating the Minuteman III spacecraft
Around 1970         — Built homemade computer from IBM 1620 parts and TI TTL logic; used SAGE magnetic drum memory and Friden Flexowriter; attempted new robot project with Caltech's Phil Roberts
PhD entry           — PhD in Physics, University of Colorado; built world's highest field optically accessible superconducting magnet system; built Alpha-Newt robot
1975                — Earned Ph.D. in Physics, University of Colorado
1976                — University of Paris exchange scientist fellowship (10 months); married; developed Beta-Newt (with onboard 8080 computer)
Around 1976         — Cover article on Newt robot in Byte Magazine — most popular article at the time
Around 1978         — Alpha/Beta-Newt featured in WGBH NOVA "The Mind Machines"; Wall Street Journal article
Around 1976         — Joined IBM Research (Yorktown Heights) — applied physics (magnetic fields, microwave acoustics)
Around 1982         — Fully transitioned to robotics at IBM; collaborated with Larry Lieberman, Russ Taylor, David Grossman, and others
IBM years           — Developed Fine Positioner (coarse/fine positioning, IBM Annual Report cover); air-bearing 3-DOF Fine Positioner; precision assembly using SCARA robot
Mid-to-late 1980s   — Developed Magic Wrist based on magnetic levitation — collaborated with Tim Salcudean and Peter Allen; Lorentz force-based 6-DOF levitation; built 4 units
1987                — Presented Magic Wrist at ISRR Santa Cruz (before demo); skeptical question from Bernie Roth
Subsequently        — Promoted to IBM Advanced Robotics manager; researched RobotWorld and Minifactory based on Sawyer planar motor
1992                — IBM Research downsized 400 staff — accepted buyout
1993                — Joined Carnegie Mellon University Robotics Institute (Smith Hall); transported 65 boxes / 40,000 pounds of IBM equipment; students Peter Berkelman and Arthur Quaid joined
CMU years           — Minifactory project (NSF-funded, ongoing for 14 years, 48 publications); built 10 magnetic levitation haptic devices (NSF MRI grant)
Around 2007         — Founded Butterfly Haptics, LLC ("less than 3 years ago" at the time of interview)
Subsequent research — Ballbot — world's first dynamically stabilized mobile robot on a single spherical wheel; joint research with Roberta Klatzky (psychology)
November 22, 2010   — Interviewed by Selma Šabanović and Peter Asaro (Pittsburgh)
```
