# Petar Kokotovic — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/kokotovic.md`)

```
1934            — Born in Belgrade, Yugoslavia
Childhood       — Schooling in Belgrade interrupted by World War II
                 — Family home destroyed in bombing; four years without schooling
Postwar         — Education resumed with schools running 3–4 shifts; Belgrade provided high-quality education
Around 1952–1957 — Studied electrical engineering at the University of Belgrade (highly competitive admissions; qualifying exam after two years)
Undergrad years — Introduced to automatic control under Prof. Mitrović's influence (servo systems lectures)
                 — Worked on heavy-water level and control of an experimental reactor at the Boris Pupin Institute's nuclear science research division
                 — Joined the early days of Rajko Tomović's Belgrade Arm project (later continued by Vukobratović, supported by the U.S. Veterans Administration)
During undergrad — Industrial internships in France (an automated power plant) and Germany (a Stuttgart factory)
Around 1957     — Bachelor's/Master's degrees delayed (study abroad to avoid military service)
1960            — Attended the Moscow IFAC Congress (traveled by train with colleagues from Belgrade, Zagreb, and Sarajevo)
                 — Belgrade Hand presented at the same conference; witnessed Russia's Kobrinsky demonstrate an EMG-controlled prosthetic arm
Around 1960     — Hosted Norbert Wiener at the Opatija cybernetics conference; gave him a tour of Belgrade
Decision to study abroad — Lost his Fulbright candidacy due to a Mercedes joke incident
                 — Encouraged to leave with a one-year fellowship in Moscow
                 — Enrolled at the Moscow Academy of Sciences IAT (Institute of Automation and Telemechanics)
                 — Initially gained informal access to the closed institute through Prof. Feldbaum, eventually formalized
PhD             — PhD from the Moscow IAT
                 — Formed ties with Soviet colleagues Aizerman, Tsypkin, Emelyanov, Utkin, and others (the "Magnificent Seven")
After return    — Brief stay in Belgrade; decided to move to the U.S. (a period when the U.S., post-Sputnik, was recruiting Russian control-theory talent)
1965            — Joined the University of Illinois (Urbana), USA (Mac Van Valkenburg served as a mentor)
                 — 50/50 joint appointment in Coordinated Science Lab and the Department of Electrical Engineering
Around 1965–1967 — Interactions with Heinz von Foerster and Ross Ashby
Around 1973–1974 — Attended the Apollo Soyuz follow-up meeting (at a sports training center near Yerevan)
                 — Active with Western colleagues such as Jurgen Ackerman
Illinois years  — Formed the "Illinois Trio" with Bill Perkins and Joe Cruz
                 — Bridged East-West exchanges, e.g., inviting Soviet colleague Utkin to Urbana for a year
                 — One of his students worked on the Scheinman Hand; Mark Spong worked on small robots (Pendubot/Acrobot) and textbook writing
1965–1990       — 25 years at Illinois
1990            — Moved to UC Santa Barbara when his wife Anna was appointed there
                 — Director, Center for Control Engineering and Computation
At time of interview — Professor Emeritus, UC Santa Barbara; recipient of the IEEE Control Systems Award; IEEE Life Fellow
```
