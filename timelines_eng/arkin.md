# Ronald Arkin — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/arkin.md`)

```
1949            — Born in Manhattan, New York City; raised in Roslyn, Long Island
Undergrad       — BS in Chemistry with a double major in Applied Mathematics at the University of Michigan
Graduate school — MS in Organic Chemistry from Stevens Institute of Technology
After MS        — Worked as a chemist at a dye company and in pharmaceutical R&D
Late 1970s      — Moved with his wife to New Hampshire; bought a 50-acre property
About 10 years  — Taught chemistry at Hawthorne College (Antrim, NH), later acquired by Florida Institute of Technology
Mid-teaching    — Volunteered to launch a Computer Science program based on his applied math background; served as department chair
Just before PhD — Took a robotics course at the University of Lowell that fit his schedule
Entering PhD    — Joined the Visions Lab at UMass Amherst; advisor Ed Riseman; mentored by Michael Arbib
During PhD      — Quit his full-time job to focus on the degree; commuted weekly to support his family
1987            — Earned PhD in Computer Science from UMass Amherst (in 3 years); thesis on hybrid deliberative-reactive architectures and schema theory; used the HARV robot (Denning No. 2)
After 1987      — Joined Georgia Tech (28 years there), the Computer Science group's first robotics professor
Mid-1990s       — First sabbatical: one year at KTH Sweden with Henrik Christensen, helping found the Center for Autonomous Systems
Late 1990s      — Expanded into multi-robot cooperation and formation control (NSF, DARPA funding)
Early 2000s     — Began consulting for Sony (10 years); developed and patented ethological/emotional architectures for AIBO and QRIO
Second sabbatical — Four months at Sony Japan, then about a year at Toulouse CNRS LAAS (collaborating with Raja Chatila and Rachid Alami)
2003            — Elected IEEE Fellow
Mid-2000s       — Recruited Henrik Christensen to Georgia Tech; the RIM Center evolved into the IRIM Institute
Date unknown    — ~$300K Army Research Office grant for ethical-architecture research on lethal autonomy: ethical governor, ethical adapter, responsibility advisor
2010            — Selected for Time magazine's "Top 50 Inventions of 2010" for work including squirrel deception research
2014            — At time of interview: Regents' Professor at Georgia Tech, Director of the Mobile Robot Lab, Associate Dean of the College of Computing
At interview    — 5-year NSF grant with Tufts (Matthias Scheutz) on robots for early-stage Parkinson's care; DTRA project on robots for WMD response
```
