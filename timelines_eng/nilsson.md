# Nils Nilsson — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/nilsson.md`)

```
1933              — Born in Saginaw, Michigan, USA
Around 1944       — Moved with family to California at age 11
High school years — Attended high school in Glendale, California
Undergrad years   — Entered Stanford University, majoring in Electrical Engineering (specializing in information theory)
1956              — First-year graduate student at Stanford, began research in information theory
During MS         — Member of Air Force ROTC, enrolled in 5-year MS program without a separate BS
1958              — Earned PhD from Stanford (advisor Willis Harmon, research on game-theoretic radar reception)
Right after PhD   — Joined the US Air Force, served three years at Rome Air Development Center in New York (monitoring radar/pulse compression research)
After Air Force   — Joined SRI (Stanford Research Institute), joined Charlie Rosen's Learning Machines Group, began neural network (perceptron) research
Early 1960s       — Interacted with Frank Rosenblatt, developed perceptron-variant systems at SRI
Mid-1960s         — Pattern recognition research with Army Signal Corps support, including handwritten FORTRAN coding sheet recognition
1963–1964         — Charlie Rosen wrote the SHAKEY (automaton) memo, secured ARPA funding
1968              — Seconded for half a year from SRI to the Stanford AI Lab on Arastradero Road
SHAKEY era        — Co-developed the A* algorithm and the STRIPS automatic planning system, established the 3-layer architecture
Around 1971–1972  — SHAKEY film shot, SHAKEY project effectively ended
1970s             — After ARPA cut robotics funding, attempted the Computer Based Consultant project, then shifted to natural-language database access (TEAM, etc.)
Late 1970s        — Developed the Prospector expert system with Peter Hart and Dick Duda (successful prediction of a molybdenum deposit, published in Science)
1980              — Became director of SRI AI Center after Peter Hart's departure
1985              — Appointed chair of the Stanford Computer Science Department
1985–1991         — Served a 6-year term as Stanford department chair
Fall 1990–        — Sabbatical: visited MIT (Rod Brooks's lab), Harvard, and the Santa Fe Institute
Through summer 1991 — Developed the teleo-reactive program concept, worked with students on simulated robots and software agents
2011              — Inducted into the IEEE Intelligent Systems' AI Hall of Fame
June 14, 2011     — ETHW oral history interview with Peter Asaro (Medford, Oregon)
```
