# Oussama Khatib — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/khatib.md`)

```
Birth           — Born in Aleppo, Syria; grew up there
School years    — Attempted to enroll in the Engineering Faculty at Aleppo University; turned down medical school after his father accompanied him to enroll in engineering
                 — Soon went to France on a French government scholarship
Undergrad years — Studied automatic control and electrical engineering at the University of Montpellier in southern France
1971            — B.S. (Electrical Engineering), University of Montpellier
1974            — M.S. (Electrical Engineering), University of Montpellier
Graduate years  — Moved to SUPAERO (National Higher School of Aeronautics and Space) in Toulouse, beginning with an AI project
                 — In the absence of an advisor, independently developed the artificial potential field idea
1976            — Advanced Degree (Automatic Control)
1978            — Presented his first paper on artificial potential fields in Italy; met Stanford's Prof. Bernard Roth
1980            — PhD from SUPAERO (Automatic Systems, "Operational Space Control")
Right after PhD — Returned to Montpellier and implemented potential fields on a nuclear power plant robot (MA23, designed by Jean Vertut)
1981            — Began a postdoc at Stanford at the invitation of Roth (the catalyst for permanently settling there)
1981–1989       — Senior/Research Associate, Stanford CS Department
Early Stanford  — Joined the AI Lab (alongside John Craig, Tom Binford, Rodney Brooks, Ken Salisbury, etc.)
                 — Implemented torque control directly on a Puma robot after its cables were cut
1983            — First trip to Japan (interactions with Inoue, Hirose, Nakamura, Hanafusa, Yoshikawa, etc.)
Around 1983     — Demonstrations of compliant motion, double peg-in-hole assembly, and other force control demos
1984            — Attended his first ICRA (Atlanta)
1985            — First official ICRA presentation (St. Louis), on artificial potential fields
Mid-to-late 1980s — Retrofitted a torque sensor on Puma joint 3; demonstrated torque control capability
Late 1980s      — Artisan robot project (full torque control with inductive torque sensing and LVDT)
1989            — Co-founded ISER (International Symposium of Experimental Robotics) with Vincent Hayward (McGill) in Montreal
1989, 1991      — Co-edited Robotics Review (MIT) with John Craig and Tomás Lozano-Pérez
1990–1999       — Associate Professor, Stanford CS (joint with Mechanical Engineering)
1990s           — Developed the Romeo & Juliet mobile manipulator platform (cooperative tasks and human-robot collaboration)
Early 1990s     — Began haptics research (collision detection based on sphere hierarchies)
                 — Elastic planning / Bubble band concepts (with students Sean Quinlan, Oliver Brock, etc.)
1996            — Received the Japan Robot Association (JARA) Award
1997–1999       — Director, Stanford Computer Forum
1999            — Began collaboration on the Honda ASIMO project (humanoid manipulation research)
2000            — Promoted to Full Professor at Stanford
2000            — Chair of ICRA San Francisco (introduced mini-symposia and digest concepts; the last paper-submission conference)
2000s           — Developed the Position-to-Torque Transformer for compliant control of ASIMO
                 — DM2 (Distributed Macro-Mini Actuation) safe robot project (with Mike Zinn, Dongjun Shin, Roth, Salisbury)
                 — Analyzed human musculoskeletal models (discovery of E = ΣCM²)
2003            — Elected IEEE Fellow
Around 2009     — Started an fMRI-compatible haptic device project
2010            — Received the IEEE RAS Pioneer Award in Robotics and Automation
2011            — IROS San Francisco (about 2,000 attendees)
2012            — Co-Chair, RoManSy (Paris)
2013            — Received the IEEE RAS Distinguished Service Award
                — Co-edited the Springer Handbook of Robotics with Bruno Siciliano (1,600 pages, ~160 contributors)
At time of interview — Director of the Stanford Robotics Lab; President of IFRR
                 — New projects underway including a humanoid diver (aquanaut) for Red Sea coral reef exploration and Supra-ped
```
