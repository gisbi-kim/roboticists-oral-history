# Makoto Kaneko — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/kaneko.md`)

```
1954.01.18      — Born in Hagi, Yamaguchi Prefecture, Japan (a small city of 50,000)
Childhood       — Fascinated by nature, fish, and insects; built his own toys
Undergrad years — Mechanical engineering at Kyushu Institute of Technology
1978            — Entered the University of Tokyo graduate school (to study under a renowned professor)
Graduate years  — Specialized in fluid dynamics under Prof. Hideo Ohashi (research on nuclear power plant pump systems)
1981            — PhD in Mechanical Engineering, University of Tokyo (clarification of pump vibration phenomena)
1981            — Joined the Mechanical Engineering Laboratory (MEL, later AIST) under MITI by passing the entrance exam; switched specialty to robotics
Early MEL years — Joined the Guide Dog Robot project as a newcomer (collaborating with Drs. Tachi, Komorie, Tanie, and others)
2–3 years after joining MEL — Began designing a six-legged walking robot at his supervisor's direction (designing the body to remain horizontal)
1986            — Started research on multi-fingered robot hands; invented a new pulse sensor (Japanese and U.S. patents)
1988–1989       — Postdoctoral researcher at TU Darmstadt, Germany (space robotics project; presented the idea of contact point detection without tactile sensors)
1990–1994       — Technical Editor, IEEE Transactions on Robotics and Automation
1990–1993       — Associate Professor, Computer Science and Systems Engineering, Kyushu Institute of Technology
1990            — Research on the "Active Antenna" tactile sensor inspired by insect antennae (cited in Nature)
1991.11–1992.01 — Visiting Professor, TU Darmstadt, Germany
1991–           — Member of the IEEE IROS Program Committee
1993.10–2006.09 — Professor of Industrial Engineering, Hiroshima University
1996            — First final design of the multi-fingered robot hand
1998            — Program Chair, International Conference on Advanced Mechatronics
2002–2003       — Joined as Associate Professor specializing in high-speed vision; established the "Hyper Human" research direction
2003            — Completed the final design of the multi-fingered robot hand
Around 2003     — Developed a 100g-acceleration capture robot ("Too Fast to See" catchphrase)
2004–2005       — Vice President, IEEE Robotics and Automation Society
2005            — Began expanding into medical applications (palpation, stiffness measurement)
2006            — Elected IEEE Fellow
2006.10–        — Professor, Department of Mechanical Engineering, Osaka University
At time of interview — Director of the Hyper Human Research Project Center, focusing on medical applications
```
