# John Craig — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/craig.md`)

```
Around 1967         — Fascinated by robots after seeing one his brother built (childhood)
Around 1971         — Built a plywood box-style walking robot for a high school science fair
Early 1970s         — Entered RPI (Troy, NY), majoring in electrical engineering
Mid-1970s           — Joined the RPI Mars rover project (late undergrad through 5th-year master's)
1976~77             — Handled Mars rover electronics under Steve Yerazunis; supported filming of Carl Sagan's "Cosmos" in Death Valley
Around 1977         — Joined JPL after interviews at Procter & Gamble and others
1977~78             — Joined JPL; worked on a 6-axis force sensor and the Scheinman arm with Marc Raibert as mentor
1978                — Pin-in-hole experiment with the Scheinman arm (hybrid position/force control)
1979                — Published the hybrid position/force control paper with Marc Raibert
1979~80             — Second year at JPL; researched a robot programming language (JARS) with Carl Ruoff
1981                — Published a second force control paper with Marc Raibert
Early 1980s         — Entered the Stanford PhD program under Tom Binford; collaborated on software for Ken Salisbury's 3-finger hand
Around 1983~84      — Taught Stanford's robotics course in place of Bernie Roth's sabbatical (the first robotics course offering)
1983~84             — Worked at the GM Tech Center (Warren, MI) over the summer; wrote an adaptive control paper
Around 1984         — Switched advisors from Tom Binford to Bernie Roth
1986                — Earned PhD from Stanford; published the textbook "Introduction to Robotics" (Addison Wesley)
Around 1986         — Declined a Berkeley faculty offer after the PhD; co-founded Silma with colleagues
Around 1986         — Founded Silma (3D robot simulation / offline programming company), collaborating with Chris Goad
After 1986          — Published the PhD dissertation as the book "Adaptive Control of Manipulators"
Mid-1990s           — Silma was acquired by Adept Robotics (reuniting with Brian Carlisle and Bruce Shimano)
Adept years         — Collaborated with Ken Goldberg; developed the Rapid product (parts feeding, vision integration)
After Adept         — Left Adept and joined Invenios (Santa Barbara), developing high-precision encoder technology
Invenios years      — Developed a DSP-based million-count-per-revolution encoder and the IRS (Invenios Robot System) language (about 6 years)
Around 2009         — Left Invenios; ran an animal radiology imaging software business and his wife's polyurethane horseshoe company
2010-11-19          — IEEE oral history interview in Monterey, CA
```
