# Bruce Shimano — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/shimano.md`)

```
Birth                 — Born and raised in Los Angeles, California
1967                  — Entered Stanford University (undergraduate, Mechanical Engineering)
Sophomore year        — Assigned as Bernard Roth's student (advised through undergrad to PhD)
1972                  — Earned combined B.S. + M.S. (Computer Graphics) at Stanford in 5 years
1972~1974             — Left Stanford for 2 years of outside activity
1974                  — Returned to Stanford for PhD; joined the SAIL (Stanford AI Lab) Hand-Eye Group under Lou Paul
SAIL period           — Joined development of the AL (originally HAL) language; shared an office with Victor Scheinman
SAIL period           — Worked alongside Tom Binford (vision), Bob Bolles, Ray Finkel, and others
Around 1976~1977      — Developed VAL test software part-time at Victor Scheinman's Vicarm for GM
Around 1978~1979      — Finished SAIL PhD coursework, joined Vicarm/Unimation full-time (resided in LA)
Around 1978~1979      — First integrated demo with the SRI/Machine Intelligence vision system at Robots 9 conference
Around 1979           — Vicarm sold to Unimation; the company became Unimation's western division
Around 1979~1980      — Won the GM RFQ; began development of PUMA (Programmable Universal Machine for Assembly)
Around 1979~1980      — Delivered the first PUMA prototype within about 18 months; Brian Carlisle handled tech transfer to Danbury
Around 1980           — Earned Ph.D. in Mechanical Engineering from Stanford
Around 1980~1981      — First PUMA introduced into a GM plant
December 1982         — Sale of Unimation to Westinghouse completed
June 1983             — Co-founded Adept Technology with Brian Carlisle (minority Westinghouse stake + venture capital); VP of R&D
June 1985             — Demonstrated Adept 1 (direct-drive SCARA)
Around 1986~1987      — Elaine Wood led development of fiducial vision applications for PCB odd-form components
Late 1980s            — Adept 1 in mass production; established the U.S. assembly robot market
Early 1990s           — Many U.S. industrial robot companies fell away; Adept remained essentially the sole U.S. robot company
1996                  — Adept Technology IPO
Early 2000s           — Made a large bet on the photonics market
9/11/2001             — Photonics collapse + 9/11 reduced revenue from $100M to $30-40M; staff cut by two-thirds
December 2003         — Resigned alongside Brian Carlisle following Adept management changes (VP R&D and secretary)
2004                  — Co-founded Precise Automation LLC with Brian Carlisle; COO and VP of R&D
Precise period        — Small-organization + outsourced manufacturing partner model; focus on lab automation/pharma/semiconductors/packaging
At interview          — Operating Precise Automation
November 12, 2010     — ETHW oral history interview with Peter Asaro in Los Angeles
```
