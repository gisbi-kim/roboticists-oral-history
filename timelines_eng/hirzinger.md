# Gerd Hirzinger — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/hirzinger.md`)

```
1945              — Born in Schwandorf, Bavaria, Germany
Childhood/school  — School years in Amberg, northern Bavaria
1969              — Earned electrical engineering degree (Dipl.-Ing.) from Technical University of Munich; joined DLR — initially worked on interferometry and high-frequency techniques
Early DLR years   — Switched to control theory after 6 months; began PhD on digital control (process computers like PDP-11); satellite attitude control, air-bearing tables, optimal digital control of unstable aircraft (CCV)
1974              — Earned PhD at TU Munich — digital control
1976              — Became leader of DLR Automation group
1976~1977         — Brainstormed applications of digital control to mechanical systems — considered manipulators, underwater, prosthetics; after visiting BMW's Wolfgang Reitzle, received an ABB robot as donation
1978              — Demonstrated automated assembly of BMW oil pump with first force-torque sensor at Hannover Fair
Late 1970s        — Developed first force-torque sensor (presumed first in Europe); implemented coordinate transformation on a 4-bit microcontroller
1982              — Bought second ABB robot; developed cooperative control of two robots and a control ball interface
1985              — Presented at IEEE St. Louis conference → recognized by Mike Brady and others; first ISRR (Goviller, France) invitation; first meeting with Oussama Khatib — torque control inspiration
Around 1987       — Accepted proposal for ROTEX robot experiment on Spacelab D2 shuttle mission; began conceptualizing lightweight robot
1991              — Concurrent professorship at TU Munich
1991/1992         — Completed first lightweight robot — for astronaut training
1992              — Became Director of Institute of Robotics and Mechatronics Center (RMC)
April 1993        — Spacelab D2 mission launched, ROTEX experiment — succeeded in 6~7 second-delay teleoperation, autonomous grasping of floating objects; Leibniz Award (1.5 million euros) right after ROTEX → began medical robotics research
1994              — Began full-scale collaboration with KUKA — provided real-time robot dynamics models (adopted by BMW and others)
Mid-to-late 1990s — Optimized RoboDrive motor series via simulation (about 2 years)
Late 1990s        — KUKA won competitive contracts at BMW, Mercedes, and Ford (Hirzinger group contributed); founded SensoDrive, Dualis, and other spin-offs; Logitech acquired Space Mouse
1997              — Japan's ETS-VII free-flying robot launched (Dr. Oda's team)
1999              — Visited Tsukuba, Japan, remotely controlled space robot ETS-VII from the ground; satellite attitude reorientation experiment
Early 2000s       — Completed third-generation lightweight robot (carbon fiber, RoboDrive motors)
Around 2005       — Began ROKVISS experiment outside the space station — 2 joints, 20ms delay telepresence
2010              — IEEE Fellow
May 2011          — ROKVISS system retrieved from space (landed in Kazakhstan); 6-year space exposure analysis to be conducted
July 20, 2011     — Interviewed by Peter Asaro (Wessling, Germany)
Ongoing research  — MiroSurge medical robot, ATHLETE collaboration (JPL), Mars/lunar rovers (Part-Time Scientists team), artificial heart (Dualis), variable stiffness hand-arm system
```
