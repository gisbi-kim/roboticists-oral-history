# Maria Gini — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/gini.md`)

```
Birth                — Born in Italy (with twin sister Giuseppina)
High school          — Classical studies track (science, math, physics + philosophy)
Undergrad            — Majored in physics in Italy (no computer science degree at the time); studied alongside her sister
Undergrad years      — Drawn to cybernetics after being inspired by Maturana's "What the Eye of the Frog Tells the Brain of the Frog" paper
After undergrad      — Joined Professor Marco Somalvico's group at Politecnico di Milano (AI research); continued joint research with her sister
1976~1977            — Fellowship at Stanford AI Lab (Arastradero Road) with her sister; entered robotics under Tom Binford
Stanford years       — Developed the interactive programming system POINTY in the blue/yellow Scheinman arm and AL language environment (extending an idea by Russ Taylor and Raphael Finkel)
Stanford years       — Interacted with Shahid Mujtaba, Ron Goldman, Vic Scheinman, Bruce Shimano, Hans Moravec, Rodney Brooks, David Lowe, etc.
After 1977           — Returned to Italy due to J1 visa requirements; continued research at Politecnico di Milano
1981                 — Husband earned his PhD from Stanford and received a Minnesota faculty offer
1982                 — Faculty appointment in Computer Science at the University of Minnesota (the department's first woman, the only one out of 14)
Early 1980s          — First PhD student worked on assembly program error detection/recovery (using Max Donath's IBM arm)
Late 1980s           — Brought in a Lab Mate mobile robot; researched navigation/obstacle avoidance/door closing
First sabbatical     — Returned to Stanford; motion planning research with Jean-Claude Latombe; developed parallel algorithms
1990s                — Parallel motion planning with Nancy and others; later shifted to multi-agent / auction-based task allocation
2000s                — Joined the Trading Agent Competition; Markov-based prediction algorithms (a student won the INFORMS award and became a professor at Rotterdam business school)
Mid-2000s            — Designed and commercialized the Scout small exploration robot (the size of a soda can) under DARPA funding
2006                 — Chair of the Distributed Autonomous Robotics Systems Conference
Around 2010          — Joined RoboCup Rescue (two appearances); researched team formation and time-cost task allocation
At time of interview — Advising students on using humanoid robots for diagnosing autistic children, and on multi-robot building exploration; teaching undergraduate AI
2015-01-22           — IEEE oral history interview in College Station, TX
```
