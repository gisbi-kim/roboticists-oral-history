# John McCarthy — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/mccarthy.md`)

```
1927-09-04        — Born in Boston, Massachusetts, USA
Childhood         — Attended Boston public schools
1947              — BS in mathematics from Caltech
Fall 1948         — Attended Caltech's Hixon Symposium; conceived of the idea of implementing intelligent behavior with computers
1948–1949         — Completed one year of graduate study at Caltech, then entered Princeton's mathematics PhD program
1949              — After entering Princeton, met with von Neumann and discussed ideas about probabilistic automata
1951              — PhD in mathematics from Princeton ("Solutions to Differential Equations")
1951–1953         — Two years as an instructor at Princeton
Summer 1952       — Summer position at Bell Labs; met Claude Shannon
Around 1953–1954  — One year as acting assistant professor at Stanford; not reappointed
Around 1954       — Moved to Dartmouth
Summer 1955       — Entered the computing field through a summer position at IBM
1955-08-31        — Wrote the Dartmouth summer research proposal, first using the term "artificial intelligence"
1956              — Held the Dartmouth summer conference (the starting point of the AI field)
September 1956    — Presented on the Dartmouth project and the state of AI at the Boston IRE/IEEE meeting
1958              — Published "Programs with Common Sense," the starting point of logical AI
Fall 1958         — Began developing the LISP programming language
Around 1958       — Moved to MIT (Sloan Foundation Fellowship, then assistant professor of Communication Sciences)
Around 1958       — Co-founded the MIT AI Lab with Marvin Minsky
Early 1959        — Wrote the LISP universal function report; Steve Russell implemented it as an interpreter
1962              — Promoted to associate professor at MIT
Spring 1962       — Received a full professor offer from George Forsythe at Stanford
Fall 1962         — Moved to Stanford as a full professor
Around 1965       — Founded the Stanford AI Lab; brought in a PDP-6 with DARPA funding
After 1965        — Supported Victor Scheinman's hydraulic and electric robot arm development
1971              — Received the Turing Award
Around 1989       — Stanford AI Lab moved onto campus; DARPA funding troubles began
1991              — Received the National Medal of Science
2000              — Retired from Stanford
2011-06-08        — IEEE History Center oral history interview (Stanford, CA)
2011-10-24        — Died from complications of heart disease (age 84)
```
