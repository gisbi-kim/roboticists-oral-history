# Richard Murray — Career Timeline

> Source: ETHW Oral History Interview (`ethw_source_eng/murray.md`)

```
Childhood         — Grew up in El Paso, Texas; attended school in El Paso through high school
1981              — Entered Caltech as an undergraduate, majoring in electrical engineering
1985              — BS in electrical engineering from Caltech
1985              — Entered graduate school at UC Berkeley; researched robotics and grasping under S. Shankar Sastry
PhD               — Built the planar manipulator "Styx" with Kris Pister
PhD               — Researched non-holonomic motion planning
1988              — MS in electrical engineering and computer science from UC Berkeley
1990              — PhD from UC Berkeley (grasping/manipulation + non-holonomic motion)
Late 1990         — Worked as a lecturer at Berkeley for six months
1991              — Returned to Caltech as associate professor of mechanical engineering; collaborated with Joel Burdick
Early-to-mid 1990s — Researched non-holonomic mobile robots and underwater swimming robots
Mid-1990s         — Expanded into UAV (unmanned aerial vehicle) dynamics and trajectory generation research
1995              — Received the NSF Early Faculty Development Award
1997              — Received the AACC Donald P. Eckman Award
1997–2000         — Associate professor at Caltech
1998–2000         — Sabbatical at United Technologies Research Center (Hartford, CT)
2000–2005         — Full professor at Caltech; served as chair of Engineering and Applied Science
Early 2000s       — Expanded into multi-vehicle / networked cooperative systems research
Around 2003       — First entry in the DARPA Grand Challenge; mobilized 60–70 undergraduates and drove 1.1 miles
2004              — Competed in the DARPA Grand Challenge main event
2005              — Second entry in the DARPA Grand Challenge; the vehicle "Alice" went over a concrete barrier
2005–2006         — Professor of Control and Dynamical Systems at Caltech
2006–2009         — Everhart Professor; chair of Information Science and Technology
Around 2007       — Competed in the DARPA Urban Challenge (passed qualifiers but did not reach the final); worked jointly with JPL
Around 2007–2008  — Proposed and won a molecular programming project under the NSF Expeditions program
2008–2009         — Served as interim chair of Engineering and Applied Science
After 2009        — Everhart Professor (Control and Dynamical Systems and Bioengineering)
2011-06-06        — IEEE History Center oral history interview (Pasadena, CA)
Later             — Collaborations in synthetic biology and DNA origami (Paul Rothemund, Erik Winfree, and others)
Later             — Human-robot interaction research, including robot navigation in cafeteria crowd environments
```
